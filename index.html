The Cost of Survival: The Lasting Problems of Premature and Dysmature Foals – Part Two – By Dr. Jane Clothier

Since researching the ongoing issues experienced by horses with a history of gestational immaturity – a group that includes former premature and dysmature foals, including twins – I notice a lot of physical signs in client horses that seem to escape general notice.

This is partly down to the fact that as bodyworkers (I would say therapists, but am aware that the term has different meanings in different countries), we explore and assess whole horse connections that are often grey areas when it comes to veterinary pathological conditions.

But it’s more than that: it’s a whole way of thinking that hasn’t yet been brought into the popular domain.

For example, we hear a lot about growth plate closure timing, but very little about ossification stages.

I believe we are failing to anticipate what amounts to a syndrome of prematurity and dysmaturity. It definitely takes time for awareness to change: unfortunately, and understandably, owners who realise that the horse they adore, and may well have spent thousands on saving, has ongoing issues,very often don’t want to hear more.

I am therefore very, very appreciative of owners who have extended conversations with me – and also for opportunities to communicate freely in forums such as this one.

Ten years ago, I commenced a research degree that started as a Master of Science but progressed to a PhD. My research was entitled “Beyond the Miracle Foal: A Study into the Persistent Effects of Gestational Immaturity in Horses”.

This is a challenging area of research due to the time factor: longitudinal studies are fraught with difficulties due to the changing experiences of individual horses.

My research was therefore based on a population of case horses that still resided with their owner-breeders, where I was also able to include related horses as controls.

There were still plenty of limitations, of course, but in a series of studies, I was able to explore a syndrome of gestational issues associated with premature and dysmature birth that I believe to be a major concern.

Developmental programming seems to be considered in just about every breed of domesticated animal except horses.

It’s astonishing really, given how much we expect our horses to do over the course of many, many years.

I was extremely fortunate to be championed by a principal animal scientist and veterinarian in the Animal Welfare and Behaviour Group of CSIRO (Australia’s national science agency).

Working in a cross-species environment was hugely beneficial, for developmental programming (or the Developmental Origins of Health and Disease – DOHaD) is well researched in production animals.
